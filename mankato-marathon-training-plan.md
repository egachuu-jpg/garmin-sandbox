# Comprehensive Mankato Marathon Training Plan
**Prepared for:** 1:45 Half-Marathoner
**Target Event:** Mankato Marathon (Saturday, October 17, 2026)
**A-Goal:** 3:50 (8:46/mile) — aerobic capacity for ~3:40–3:45 is present
**B-Goal (safety net):** Sub-4:00 (9:09/mile) — the floor to defend on a hard day
**Starting Baseline:** 25 miles/week
**Medical Constraint:** Right SI Joint Dysfunction Management

---

## Section 1: Core Pacing Framework
Your recent 1:45 half marathon demonstrates raw aerobic capacity for a ~3:40–3:45 marathon under ideal conditions. Rather than leave that on the table, the **A-goal is 3:50**, with **sub-4:00 held in reserve as a B-goal** to defend if the SI joint, heat, or a rough day intervenes. Train strictly within these zones to hit that target while protecting your right SI joint from high-impact stress:

*   **Easy & Recovery Runs:** 10:00 to 10:45 per mile. Must feel entirely conversational to prevent structural inflammation.
*   **Marathon Goal Pace (MGP) — A-goal 3:50:** 8:40 to 8:50 per mile. Your target tempo and selective long-run closeout pace.
*   **Sub-4 Floor (B-goal pace):** 9:09 per mile. The pace you fall back to if you shift goals mid-race — know how it feels.
*   **Intervals / Speedwork:** 7:40 to 8:10 per mile. Maintains fast-twitch efficiency without accumulating structural fatigue.

### Heat & Humidity Adjustment (critical — this build runs through a Minnesota summer)
The pace zones above are **cool-weather targets.** Your peak build is July–August. On warm days:

*   Add **~15–20 sec/mile per 10°F above 60°F**, and more when the dew point is above 65°F.
*   On genuinely hot/humid mornings, **run by effort and heart rate, not the pace band.** A 10:00–10:45 "easy" run at 78°F with a 70°F dew point is not easy — forcing the pace turns a recovery run into structural load.
*   Never grind an easy run into a hard effort just to defend a number. The paces come back automatically as temperatures drop in September–October.

## Section 2: SI Joint Stabilization Protocol
Perform these movements on **Monday and Tuesday mornings** right before or after your easy runs (not on other days). They target pelvic alignment, firing up the stabilizing muscles to keep your pelvis level under impact.

1.  **Resisted Clamshells:** 3 sets × 15 repetitions per side. Focuses on isolating the gluteus medius to stop lateral pelvic drops.
2.  **Single-Leg Glute Bridges:** 3 sets × 10 repetitions per side. Drives active hip extension through the gluteus maximus without irritating the lower lumbar vertebrae.
3.  **Bird-Dogs:** 3 sets × 12 repetitions per side. Move deliberately slowly. Emphasizes stable, cross-body core coordination.
4.  **Lateral Side Planks:** 3 sets × 30-second holds per side. Builds deep stability in the quadratus lumborum to support your pelvis on each stride.

**Two structural principles baked into this revision:**
- **Wednesday is always a recovery bike spin.** The Block C strength session moved to **Monday** (paired with the easy run + SI routine) so lower-body DOMS no longer lands on Thursday quality or Saturday's long run.
- **The long run is capped near ~42% of weekly volume** in the build weeks by adding a **medium-long midweek run** (Tuesday), rather than by shrinking the long run. Concentrating impact into one giant Saturday session is exactly what an SI joint tolerates worst.

## Section 4: SI Flare Contingency Protocol
The whole plan is built around this joint, so decide *now* how you'll respond to a flare — mid-flare decisions made emotionally tend to be bad ones. Grade by symptoms and act:

*   **Mild** (stiffness or awareness, no pain *during* running, ≤3/10): proceed, but drop that day's intensity to easy-only at the slow end of the range. Double down on the SI routine and Friday mobility. Do not add volume that week.
*   **Moderate** (pain during the run, altered/guarded gait, or lingering 4–6/10 afterward): **substitute, don't push.** Swap running for non-impact cross-training (bike or pool) for 2–3 days, keep the SI routine, and only return once pain-free walking and single-leg stance are clean.
*   **Severe** (sharp pain, pain at rest, radiating symptoms, or >6/10): **stop running.** Contact your PT and do not resume running until cleared.
*   **A missed long run does not get made up.** Never stack a skipped long run onto the following week. Resume at the *last distance you actually completed*, then continue the progression. Missing one long run costs you nothing meaningful; a re-injury costs you the marathon.

## Section 5: Race Fueling & Hydration
The single most common cause of a good-fitness / bad-result marathon is under-fueling. This is trained, not improvised.

*   **Practice on every long run ≥ 14 miles:** take **40–60g carbohydrate per hour** (≈ one gel every 30–40 minutes), starting by **mile 4–5** — fuel *before* you feel empty, not after. Sip fluid at regular intervals; add electrolytes on hot days.
*   **The gut is trainable.** Long runs are where you build tolerance to fueling at pace. Rotate this in from Week 8 onward so it's automatic by peak.
*   **Lock your exact race fuel by Week 11** — the specific brand, flavor, and timing you'll use on race day. **Nothing new on race day.** The Week 13 20-miler and the Week 14 half are full rehearsals of this exact plan.
*   **Carb-load (race week):** raise carbohydrate to ~7–10 g/kg bodyweight over the final 2–3 days, weighted toward the day before. Keep fat and fiber moderate to avoid GI distress. This is added carbs, not just "eat more."
*   **Race morning:** familiar breakfast 2.5–3 hours before the start; a top-off gel 10–15 min pre-gun. Know the Mankato aid-station spacing in advance and decide which stations you'll take.

## Section 6: Race Execution & Pacing Strategy
*   **A/B goal structure:** run the A-goal (8:45–8:50) but pre-commit to a decision point. Do not bank time early — the first 2 miles slightly *conservative*, then settle into MGP.
*   **Watch vs. official pace:** GPS drift and lost tangents make your watch read ~5 sec/mile faster than official pace over 26.2 miles. Run the *effort*, don't chase the watch.
*   **The mile 18–20 decision (pre-committed, not improvised):** if you're on A-goal pace and it feels controlled, hold and consider easing toward the low-8:40s in the final 10K. If pace has slipped, or the SI joint or heat is a factor, **shift cleanly to defending the sub-4 B-goal (9:09)** — protect the finish rather than fighting a fade into a blow-up.
*   **New flatter course profile:** As of early 2026, Visit Mankato unveiled [faster, flatter marathon courses](https://greatermankato.com/blog/visit-mankato-unveils-faster-flatter-courses-for-2026-mankato-marathon/) for the full and half. This removes historic hill risk, but still **shorten your stride on any downhill** to prevent shear force on the right SI joint.
*   **Monday Running Rule:** Because you run back-to-back on Mondays and Tuesdays, Monday must be your slowest session of the week (10:30–11:00/mile) to preserve your joints — especially on Block C strength Mondays.

## Section 7: Cross-Training Protocol

Four blocks slot into your weekly structure without adding running stress. Given your right SI joint condition, exercise cues are included where pelvic alignment matters.

### Block A — Daily Pre-Run Mobility (5–8 minutes)
Perform **every training day** before any run or workout. These warm up the joint surfaces and establish pelvic awareness before load.

1.  **Ankle Circles:** 10 rotations each direction per foot. Keep the heel grounded, move only from the ankle. Addresses ankle stiffness that shifts load compensation to the knee and hip.
2.  **Hip Circles (Standing):** 10 rotations each direction. Hands on hips, feet hip-width. Move slowly through the full arc.
3.  **Leg Swings — Front to Back:** 10 swings per leg. Hold a wall for balance. Let the leg be fully relaxed; the hip drives the movement.
4.  **Leg Swings — Lateral:** 10 swings per leg. Keep your pelvis level — do not let the hip hike on the swing-side leg. Directly trains SI joint stability under dynamic load.

### Block B — Post-Run Stretching (10–12 minutes)
Perform **after every run** (Monday, Tuesday, Thursday, Saturday). Hold each stretch 40–50 seconds per side — the highest-value window for tissue length work because muscles are warm.

**Hip Flexors**
1.  **Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch:** Half-kneeling lunge, back knee down. Tuck your pelvis under (posterior tilt) before leaning forward — do not let your lower back arch. Feel it at the front of the rear hip, not the lower back. Critical for reducing the anterior pelvic tilt that loads the right SI joint.
2.  **Couch Stretch (Progressive):** Rear foot propped against a wall, front foot flat. Introduce in Week 3 once base hip flexor length is established.

**Calves**
3.  **Straight-Leg Calf Stretch (Gastrocnemius):** Hands on wall, back leg straight, heel flat. 45 seconds.
4.  **Bent-Knee Calf Stretch (Soleus):** Same position, back knee slightly bent. Targets the soleus — most implicated in Achilles issues and late-race calf cramping.

**Sides and Upper Body**
5.  **Standing Oblique Stretch:** Reach one arm overhead and lean laterally. Keep both feet flat and hips squared. 40 seconds per side. Frees the quadratus lumborum, which stabilizes the pelvis on each stride.
6.  **Standing Chest and Upper Back Opener:** Interlace fingers behind your back, squeeze shoulder blades, look up slightly. 30 seconds. Counters forward shoulder posture that compresses breathing.
7.  **Cat-Cow (5–8 slow reps):** Move through the mid and upper thoracic spine — not just the lumbar. Decompresses the vertebral joints after impact loading.

### Block C — Monday Strength Session (45–55 minutes)
**Perform on Monday, after a deliberately short and easy run, in Weeks 3, 5, 6, 8, 9, 11, and 12.** Moved off Wednesday so lower-body DOMS doesn't compromise Thursday quality or the Saturday long run. Reduces to 30 minutes on Monday in Weeks 15–16 (taper). Dropped entirely in the peak-long-run week (13), the tune-up race week (14), and race week (17). Wednesday stays a recovery bike spin every week.

**Single-Leg Strength (3 sets each, 60 seconds rest between sets)**

1.  **Single-Leg Split Deadlift:** 8–10 reps per side. Stand on one leg, hinge from the hip, reach the free leg back as a counterbalance. Keep your pelvis level throughout — the working-leg hip must not drop or rotate. Bodyweight first, add a light dumbbell in the opposite hand (Weeks 6+).
2.  **Lateral (Side) Lunges:** 10–12 reps per side. Step wide, push the hips back, keep the lunging knee tracking over the toes. Pause 1 second at the bottom. Builds the hip abductors/adductors that control lateral pelvic stability.
3.  **Single-Leg Calf Raises:** 15–20 reps per side. On the edge of a step for full range. Lower slowly (3 seconds down). Add load (dumbbells) in Weeks 8+. Builds the calf/soleus endurance that prevents cramping in miles 20–26.

**Core (3 sets each)**

4.  **Plank:** 30 sec (Week 3) → 45 sec (Weeks 5–8) → 60 sec (Weeks 9+). Keep hips level and glutes engaged — a sagging plank trains the wrong pattern.
5.  **Crunches:** 15–20 reps, controlled. If you feel any sharp lower back or SI pain, substitute **Dead Bugs** (on your back, extend opposite arm and leg while keeping the lower back pressed flat) — equivalent core work with zero lumbar compression.

**Mobility Flow (8–10 minutes, at the end)**

6.  **90/90 Hip Mobility:** Both knees at 90° (one front, one side). Rotate between sides, pausing at end range 3–5 sec. 60 seconds each side. Best hip mobility work for SI joint function.
7.  **Wall Ankle Mobility:** Facing a wall, drive the knee toward it with the heel flat. 10 slow reps per side. Improves dorsiflexion, which affects gait efficiency.
8.  **Thread the Needle (Thoracic Rotation):** On hands and knees, slide one arm under your body. 8 slow reps per side. Mid-thoracic rotation without loading the lumbar or SI joint.

### Block D — Friday Active Recovery (20–25 minutes)
Fridays are rest days. Use the first 20–25 minutes for a flowing mobility session. **Do not make this a workout** — the goal is tissue hydration and joint mobility, not fatigue.

1.  **90/90 Hip Flow** (from Block C) — 2 minutes
2.  **Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch** (from Block B) — 45 seconds per side
3.  **Calf Stretch Sequence** (straight-leg then bent-knee) — 45 seconds per position per side
4.  **Seated Spinal Twist:** Sit cross-legged, hand on the opposite knee, rotate and look over your shoulder. 45 seconds per side.
5.  **Oblique Side Stretch with Overhead Reach** (from Block B) — 45 seconds per side
6.  **Upper Back Extension:** Seated, interlace fingers behind your head, gently arch the upper back over the chair back. 30 seconds, twice.
7.  **Figure-4 Hip Stretch (Piriformis):** On your back, cross one ankle over the opposite knee, pull both toward your chest. 45 seconds per side. SI-joint-safe deep glute stretch.

## Section 8: Gear & Shoe Rotation
This plan totals roughly **480 running miles**, so you will cross the 400-mile shoe-replacement threshold before race day unless your current pair is new. Worn midsoles increase shear force through the right SI joint.

*   **Track current mileage** on the Gear tab and plan the changeover for a **step-back week (Week 7 or Week 10)**, never in a peak or race-pace week.
*   **Never debut new shoes on a long run.** Break them in on short easy runs first.
*   **Race in shoes with ~30–60 broken-in miles** — enough to be trustworthy, fresh enough to hold cushioning through mile 26.
*   Consider a simple two-shoe rotation (one for easy days, one for quality/long) to spread wear and let the foam decompress between runs.
